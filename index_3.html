Patrician Chronicles

An exploration of power in Ancient Rome

Year 8 History — Ancient Rome

How to Play

Walk around Ancient Rome and talk to people

Movement

Use Arrow Keys or WASD to move around the map.

Talking to NPCs

Walk close to a character — a gold ring appears. Press SPACE to talk. Text types out letter by letter. Press SPACE again to skip to the end or advance to the next line.

Moving Between Areas

Talk to everyone in an area first. A banner will pop up when you're done. Then walk to the right edge to move to the next area. There are 3 areas total.

Summary / Assignment Content

After completing all 3 areas, a Summary screen opens automatically with your full assignment content — power, authority, continuity and change.

Assignment Summary

How is life today different from Ancient Rome?

Part A — Patricians in Ancient Rome

Who they were: Patricians were Rome's hereditary aristocracy — families who claimed descent from Rome's founders. From birth they had automatic advantages over everyone else.

Power and authority: Early in the Republic, only patricians could sit in the Senate, hold priesthoods, or command armies. Power was inherited, not earned.

Daily life: A patrician's day might start with a salutatio — clients arriving at dawn to pay respects. He'd then attend Senate meetings, conduct business in the Forum, and host dinner parties (convivium) at night. Manual labour was beneath him.

Who didn't have power: Plebeians were excluded from senior offices for centuries. Slaves had zero legal rights — they were property. Women, even in patrician families, were legally under male guardianship.

"Power in Rome was not something you earned. It was something you were born into." — Marcus, Patrician Senator

Change within Rome: Over time, wealthy plebeians gained access to offices through the Conflict of the Orders, and the class divide slowly blurred — but never fully disappeared.

Part B — Comparing to Today

Power today: In modern Australia, authority comes from elections, not birth. Every adult citizen votes equally regardless of family background.

Key differences:

  • Birth determines nothing — anyone can hold public office
  • Universal suffrage — all adults vote
  • Slavery is illegal — every person has legal rights
  • Women hold equal legal rights and can hold any office
  • Free public education for everyone, not just the wealthy
  • Laws protect everyone, not just one class

What stayed the same (continuity): We still have elected governments, written laws, courts, and citizenship. The word "senate" still exists in Australia. Roman legal principles — like innocent until proven guilty — underpin modern law.

Conclusion

Life in Ancient Rome for a patrician was defined by hierarchy and inherited authority. Power was concentrated in a tiny ruling class. Today those same questions — who decides, who counts — still matter, but the answers are completely different. Change came through centuries of struggle. Continuity exists in the structure of government itself — Rome's republican ideas directly inspired modern democracy.

Extension — What Can We Learn?

Rome's patrician system shows that when power is locked in one class that can't be challenged, things get unstable. The Republic collapsed partly because of inequality and political exclusion. Modern systems with checks, balances, and protected rights are partly a response to that failure.

"The history of Rome is the history of who gets to decide. That question never goes away."
